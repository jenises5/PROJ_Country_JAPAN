Essential Travel Tips

Cash is King

Despite being technologically advanced, Japan is still largely cash-based. Many small shops, restaurants, and even some accommodations may not accept credit cards.

Exchange money at the airport or withdraw from 7-Eleven ATMs which accept foreign cards.

JR Pass

If you plan to travel between cities, consider purchasing a Japan Rail (JR) Pass before arriving in Japan. It offers unlimited travel on JR trains, including most Shinkansen (bullet trains).

Convenience Stores

Japanese convenience stores (7-Eleven, Lawson, Family Mart) are amazing! They offer quality meals, snacks, drinks, ATMs, and even tickets for attractions.

Pocket WiFi / SIM Card

Stay connected by renting a pocket WiFi device or purchasing a temporary SIM card at the airport. Both options provide reliable internet access throughout your journey.

Tipping

There is no tipping culture in Japan. Providing excellent service is considered part of the job, and tipping can sometimes be seen as rude or confusing.

Trash & Recycling

Public trash bins are rare in Japan. Carry a small bag for your trash until you find a bin (usually at convenience stores or train stations). Japanese recycling is also very specific.

Best Time to Visit

Spring (March-May)

Cherry blossom season! Mild temperatures make this the most popular time to visit. The sakura (cherry blossoms) typically bloom from late March to early April.

Highlights: Cherry blossom festivals, Hanami (flower viewing parties)

Summer (June-August)

Hot and humid with frequent rainfall in early summer (rainy season). Great for hiking in northern regions and attending vibrant summer festivals.

Highlights: Summer festivals, fireworks displays, climbing Mt. Fuji

Autumn (September-November)

Comfortable temperatures and stunning autumn foliage. Second most popular season after spring.

Highlights: Autumn leaf viewing, harvest festivals, perfect hiking weather

Winter (December-February)

Cold in most regions with snow in the north. Great for winter sports and seeing snow-covered landscapes.

Highlights: Skiing/snowboarding, winter illuminations, hot springs, fewer tourists

Cultural Etiquette

  • Shoes Off: Remove shoes before entering homes, ryokans (traditional inns), and some restaurants
  • Bowing: The traditional greeting, though handshakes are becoming more common with foreigners
  • Chopsticks: Never stick chopsticks upright in rice (funeral symbolism) or pass food directly from chopsticks to chopsticks
  • Public Behavior: Talking loudly in public, especially on phones on public transport, is frowned upon
  • Onsen Etiquette: Shower thoroughly before entering hot springs and don't wear swimwear in traditional onsens

Must-Try Experiences

  • Stay in a Ryokan: Traditional Japanese inn with tatami floors and futon beds
  • Onsen Bath: Relax in natural hot springs
  • Tea Ceremony: Experience the traditional art of preparing matcha green tea
  • Izakaya Dining: Japanese pub-style eateries perfect for trying various dishes
  • Karaoke: Sing your heart out in private karaoke rooms
  • Wearing Yukata/Kimono: Many tourist areas offer rental services
